account_balance What is GST?

Goods and Services Tax (GST) is a unified indirect tax introduced in India on 1st July 2017. It replaced multiple cascading taxes levied by the central and state governments. GST is levied on the supply of goods and services and is designed to create a single national market.

Key Benefits:

  • Reduction in overall tax burden on goods, estimated at 25%-30%.
  • Creation of a unified national market.
  • Boost to the 'Make in India' initiative by making domestic goods and services more competitive.
  • Prevention of cascading taxes through input tax credit.
  • Harmonization of laws, procedures, and tax rates across India.

history History of GST in India

2000

The Vajpayee government first proposed the concept of GST in 2000. A committee led by the then Finance Minister Yashwant Sinha was formed to draft the framework. The idea was to replace the complex web of indirect taxes like excise duty, VAT, and service tax with a single, unified system. This was considered a major reform to modernize India’s taxation system and align it with global best practices. Although the process faced several challenges, this initiative marked the beginning of India’s journey towards GST.

2006

In the 2006 Budget Speech, Finance Minister P. Chidambaram announced that GST would be introduced by April 1, 2010. The Empowered Committee of State Finance Ministers was tasked with preparing a roadmap. However, consensus between the Centre and the States proved challenging. Disputes over revenue-sharing, compensation, and state autonomy delayed implementation. Despite these challenges, the 2006 announcement ensured that GST remained a central agenda item in India’s tax reforms, paving the way for further discussions and negotiations in the years to come.

2011

The UPA government introduced the 115th Constitutional Amendment Bill in 2011 to enable the introduction of GST. The Bill sought to give both Parliament and state legislatures the power to levy GST. However, political opposition and lack of consensus between the Centre and States stalled progress. While the Bill lapsed, it reignited debates on tax reforms, highlighting the urgency of replacing multiple indirect taxes with a more efficient system. This phase was crucial in maintaining momentum towards the eventual passage of GST legislation.

2014

The NDA government led by Prime Minister Narendra Modi reintroduced GST with renewed political will. Finance Minister Arun Jaitley tabled the 122nd Constitutional Amendment Bill in December 2014. The government actively worked with states to resolve key issues, including revenue compensation and tax-sharing mechanisms. This period saw intense negotiations with state governments and opposition parties, which eventually helped build the consensus required to pass the Bill. This renewed momentum was critical in finally moving GST towards successful implementation.

2017

GST was officially launched on 1st July 2017 by the Government of India in a midnight session of Parliament. The historic event marked the unification of India into a single indirect tax regime. Four tax slabs were introduced — 5%, 12%, 18%, and 28%. Petroleum products, alcohol, and a few items were kept outside GST. The GST Council, comprising the Union Finance Minister and State Finance Ministers, was empowered to make decisions on rates and reforms. This milestone fundamentally transformed India’s taxation system.

2018

By 2018, the GST Council reduced the number of goods under the highest 28% tax slab, shifting many items to lower categories. Simplification measures such as the introduction of e-way bills for transport of goods were implemented to curb tax evasion. The Council also began discussions on rationalizing rates and improving compliance procedures. Small and medium businesses were given relief through simplified return filing and threshold exemptions. This phase focused on resolving practical challenges faced by businesses during the initial rollout of GST.

2020

The COVID-19 pandemic severely impacted GST collections. The government provided relief measures such as extended deadlines for GST return filing, late fee waivers, and reduced interest rates for delayed payments. The GST Council also discussed compensation to states for revenue shortfall, leading to borrowing mechanisms. The focus was on supporting businesses during the economic slowdown while ensuring states had sufficient funds. This phase highlighted the critical role of GST as a revenue lifeline for both the Centre and States.

2023

By 2023, GST collections consistently crossed ₹1.5 lakh crore monthly, indicating improved compliance and economic recovery. The GST Council introduced measures for digitization, including e-invoicing for large taxpayers, automated returns, and AI-driven compliance checks. The government emphasized simplification of filing processes and greater transparency. Discussions on rationalizing tax slabs and expanding the GST net continued, reflecting ongoing reforms to strengthen GST as a cornerstone of India’s indirect tax system.
